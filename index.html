Frequently Asked Questions

What is field of view in photography?

Field of view (FOV) is the angular extent of the scene captured by a camera lens, measured in degrees. A wider field of view (e.g., 14mm lens on full frame = 114° horizontal) captures more of the scene, while a narrower field of view (e.g., 200mm = 10.3°) isolates a small portion. FOV depends on two factors: focal length and sensor size.

How does crop factor affect field of view?

Crop factor describes how much smaller a sensor is relative to a 36×24mm full-frame sensor. A smaller sensor captures a smaller portion of the image circle projected by the lens, effectively narrowing the field of view. Multiply the focal length by the crop factor to get the full-frame equivalent: a 50mm lens on APS-C (1.5×) gives the same FOV as 75mm on full frame. The actual focal length doesn’t change — only the field of view does.

What is the difference between full frame and APS-C?

Full frame sensors measure 36×24mm (crop factor 1.0×). APS-C sensors are smaller: 23.5×15.6mm for Nikon/Sony (1.5× crop) and 22.2×14.8mm for Canon (1.6× crop). The same lens produces a narrower field of view on APS-C. A 35mm lens on APS-C gives roughly the same FOV as a 50mm lens on full frame.

What focal length is best for portraits?

85mm on full frame is the classic portrait focal length, offering a natural perspective with flattering compression and comfortable working distance (about 2–3 meters for a head-and-shoulders shot). 50mm works well for environmental portraits, and 135mm provides even more compression for tight headshots. On APS-C, divide by the crop factor: a 56mm lens gives the 85mm-equivalent FOV.

What focal length is best for landscape photography?

Wide-angle lenses between 14mm and 35mm (full frame) are most common for landscapes. 24mm is versatile — wide enough to capture expansive scenes without extreme distortion. 14–20mm creates dramatic wide perspectives. 35mm works for landscapes where you want to include foreground interest without exaggerating the distance between near and far elements.

What does 50mm look like on a crop sensor?

A 50mm lens on an APS-C sensor (1.5× crop) gives the same field of view as a 75mm lens on full frame. On Micro Four Thirds (2.0× crop), 50mm gives a 100mm-equivalent FOV. The lens itself doesn’t change — the sensor crops into the center of the image circle, narrowing what you see.
