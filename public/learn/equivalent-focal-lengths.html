Equivalent Focal Lengths Across Sensor Sizes

A complete conversion table showing the full-frame equivalent field of view for every common focal length across all six sensor formats.

When you put a 50mm lens on a Micro Four Thirds camera, you are not shooting at 50mm — at least not in terms of how the scene looks. The field of view matches a 100mm lens on full frame. This is the concept of equivalent focal length: the full-frame focal length that would produce the same angle of view on a given sensor.

The table below is the quickest way to look this up. Find your actual focal length in the left column, then read across to your sensor format. That number is what you are effectively shooting at, in full-frame terms.

How to read this table

Each row shows an actual focal length — the number engraved on your lens. The columns show what that focal length looks like on each sensor format, expressed as a full-frame equivalent angle of view. If you shoot on full frame, the FF column is just your focal length repeated. If you shoot on APS-C (Nikon/Sony), multiply by 1.5. If you shoot on Micro Four Thirds, multiply by 2.0. Medium format, at 0.79×, actually produces a wider field of view than the lens marking suggests.

All values are rounded to the nearest millimeter. The formula is: equivalent FL = actual FL × crop factor.

What "equivalent" actually means

Equivalent focal length describes one thing only: the angle of view. A 50mm lens on Micro Four Thirds and a 100mm lens on full frame produce the same field of view when photographing the same scene from the same distance. You could swap the two cameras and the framing would be identical.

That is all equivalence means. It does not mean the two setups are optically identical in any other way. Several important characteristics do not transfer across the equivalence relationship:

Minimum focus distance. A 50mm lens focuses to its own minimum distance regardless of which sensor it is on. Equivalence says nothing about how close you can get to your subject.

Aperture and light-gathering. f/1.8 on a 50mm lens lets in the same amount of light per unit area whether you are on full frame or Micro Four Thirds. The f-number is a ratio — it is independent of sensor size. The total light gathered by the sensor differs because the sensor area differs, which affects noise at high ISO, but the lens aperture itself is unchanged.

Optical aberrations and rendering. Distortion, chromatic aberration, flare behavior — these are properties of the glass, not the sensor. A 50mm lens renders the same way regardless of which body it is mounted on. If it has mild barrel distortion wide open, that distortion is present on every sensor it covers.

Bokeh characteristics. Out-of-focus rendering — the quality of blur, not just the amount — is a property of the lens design: aperture blade count and shape, spherical aberration tuning, optical formula. These do not change with sensor size. What changes is the effective depth of field when you account for equivalent framing, but that is a depth-of-field question, not a bokeh rendering question.

The "equivalent aperture" trap

You will sometimes see discussions of "equivalent aperture" — the idea that to fully replicate a full-frame 50mm f/1.8 shot on Micro Four Thirds, you need a 25mm f/0.9 lens (25mm for the equivalent FOV, f/0.9 to gather the same total light and produce comparable depth of field). This is mathematically valid, but it is a separate concept from equivalent focal length.

Equivalent aperture is useful for comparing total system light-gathering and depth of field across formats. Equivalent focal length is useful for comparing field of view only. Do not conflate them. When someone asks "what is the equivalent of a 50mm on Micro Four Thirds?" the expected answer is 100mm — meaning the full-frame focal length that matches the FOV. They are not asking about aperture equivalence unless they say so explicitly.

The table above shows focal length equivalents only. Apply your own crop factor to aperture only when you are specifically comparing depth of field or total sensor illuminance across formats.

Practical examples from the table

Here are a few readings from the table that come up often in real-world photography discussions:

"I shoot with a 50mm on Micro Four Thirds — that's like shooting 100mm on full frame." Correct. The M4/3 column for 50mm reads 100mm. You are effectively in portrait-telephoto territory, not standard-lens territory. If you want a standard 50mm equivalent on M4/3, you need a 25mm lens.

"My 300mm f/4 on APS-C gives me 450mm equivalent reach." Correct — 300mm × 1.5 = 450mm. APS-C wildlife photographers get meaningful extra reach at no additional cost. On a 1" sensor, the same 300mm lens would be off the bottom of this table, but the principle extends: 300mm × 2.7 = 810mm equivalent.

"My medium format 80mm behaves like a 63mm on full frame." Correct — 80mm × 0.79 = 63mm. Medium format lenses produce wider fields of view than their focal length marking implies, relative to full frame. A 45mm lens on medium format at 0.79× reads as approximately 36mm equivalent — close to a classic 35mm wide angle.

"I want to shoot 35mm equivalent on Canon APS-C." Divide by the crop factor: 35 ÷ 1.6 = 21.9mm. You need approximately a 22mm lens. The 24mm f/2.8 pancake Canon makes gives you a 38mm equivalent — slightly longer than 35mm, but close.

Using the table for lens selection

The table is most useful when you are switching systems or buying a new lens and need to understand how your existing focal lengths will translate.

If you are moving from full frame to APS-C (Nikon/Sony), read the APS-C N/S column next to your current focal lengths. Your 24mm becomes a 36mm equivalent — it was wide, now it is standard. Your 35mm becomes 53mm — close to a standard lens. Your 85mm portrait lens becomes a 128mm short telephoto.

If you are moving from APS-C to full frame, work backwards. You currently shoot a 35mm for portraits (53mm equivalent) — on full frame you would reach for a 50mm or 55mm instead. Your 10–20mm ultra-wide zoom maps to roughly a 15–30mm equivalent on full frame.

For telephoto work, the crop advantage is real. A 400mm f/5.6 on a 1" sensor gives you 1080mm equivalent reach — the table's bottom-right cell. That is extraordinary reach in a manageable physical package. Bird and wildlife photographers using 1" sensor cameras exploit this constantly.

At the wide end, the calculus reverses. To shoot 14mm equivalent on a 2.7× sensor you would need a lens wider than 5mm — practically impossible to find and optically challenging to correct. This is why truly wide photography is harder on smaller sensors: the physical lens focal lengths required become impractically short.
