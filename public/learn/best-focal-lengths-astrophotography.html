Best Focal Lengths for Astrophotography

Sky coverage, exposure limits, coma, and aperture — how to choose the right focal length for shooting the Milky Way and night sky.

Astrophotography without a tracker is governed by a single hard constraint: the Earth rotates. Stars move across the sensor during your exposure, and past a certain point they stop looking like points and start looking like short streaks. Everything else — aperture, ISO, sensor size, focal length choice — is an attempt to collect enough photons before that moment arrives.

That constraint shapes lens selection for night sky work in ways that have no equivalent in daytime photography. Ultra-wide focal lengths are not just stylistically appropriate for astrophotography — they are often technically necessary. This guide covers the three focal lengths most useful for Milky Way and wide-field night sky work on a full-frame sensor, the exposure limits each allows, and the optical compromises you will encounter.

Quick reference: focal lengths for astrophotography

Focal Length Horizontal FOV (FF) 500 Rule — FF 500 Rule — APS-C (1.5×) Notes
14mm 114.2° ~35.7s ~23.8s Full Milky Way arc possible; strong coma at fast apertures
20mm 94.5° ~25s ~16.7s Best all-round balance; premium lenses control coma well
24mm 84.1° ~20.8s ~13.9s Tighter compositions; planetary conjunctions; sharper corners

The 500 rule is an approximation. Results vary with pixel density, display size, and viewing distance. The NPF rule (accounting for sensor resolution and declination) is more precise but significantly more complex to calculate in the field.

14mm — 114.2° horizontal: maximum sky coverage

At 14mm, your horizontal field of view is 114.2° — wider than natural human peripheral vision. On a full-frame sensor, this is wide enough to capture the entire visible arc of the Milky Way in a single frame on the right night from the right location. No other commonly available rectilinear focal length comes close to this coverage.

The appeal is obvious: when the subject is the whole sky, 14mm gives you the whole sky. Galactic center, surrounding star fields, and the foreground landscape all fit comfortably in one frame. For any shot where the visual statement is about the overwhelming scale of the night sky relative to the human landscape below it — a lone tree, a tent glowing orange, a ridge of mountains — 14mm gives you the spatial relationship that makes that statement possible.

The exposure limit under the 500 rule on full frame is approximately 35.7 seconds (500 ÷ 14 ≈ 35.7). This is one of the most permissive limits in wide-angle astrophotography, and it matters: those extra seconds directly translate to more light reaching the sensor, which means lower ISO, less noise, or both. On an APS-C body with a 1.5× crop factor, the effective focal length is 21mm, and the limit drops to about 23.8 seconds.

The serious limitation of fast 14mm lenses is coma. Coma is an optical aberration that causes off-axis point sources — stars, in this context — to smear into a comet-like shape rather than remaining a clean point. At f/2.8 on a budget 14mm, corner stars can look like small seagulls. This is particularly damaging in astrophotography because the whole point is to render stars as sharp points across the entire frame.

The Samyang/Rokinon 14mm f/2.8 is the canonical budget option in this focal length, widely used precisely because there are few alternatives at the price point. It produces acceptable results at f/2.8 if you are willing to accept some corner coma, and it improves noticeably when stopped down to f/4. Higher-end options — the Sigma 14mm f/1.8 DG HSM Art, or manufacturer primes from Nikon and Sony — control coma better and offer faster maximum apertures, but at substantially higher cost.

20mm — 94.5° horizontal: the best all-round choice

The 20mm focal length is where the trade-offs of astrophotography lens selection balance most cleanly. The 94.5° horizontal field of view is still very wide — wide enough to show the galactic core with substantial surrounding context, or to frame a section of the Milky Way with a meaningful landscape below it. The 500-rule limit on full frame is approximately 25 seconds, which remains generous for a fixed tripod setup. On APS-C at 1.5×, that limit falls to about 16.7 seconds — tighter, but still workable at fast apertures.

The more important reason to consider 20mm over 14mm is optical quality. Designing a fast ultra-wide lens with good coma correction is extremely difficult, and the difficulty scales with how wide the lens must be. The optical engineering challenge at 20mm is significantly more tractable than at 14mm, and it shows: several modern 20mm lenses produce excellent star shapes across much or all of the frame even at wide-open apertures.

The Sigma 20mm f/1.4 DG DN Art is the reference lens for mirrorless astrophotography in this range. Its f/1.4 maximum aperture is a full stop faster than f/2, meaning it collects twice as much light — roughly equivalent to a full stop of ISO reduction for the same exposure time. Combined with strong coma correction at wide apertures, it has become the standard recommendation for serious Milky Way shooters. The Nikon Z 20mm f/1.8 S occupies similar territory for the Nikon Z system, with well-controlled aberrations and excellent center sharpness wide open.

If budget is a concern, the 20mm range has fewer ultra-cheap options than 14mm — but the quality floor is higher. A slower, well-corrected 20mm f/2.8 will often outperform a fast, poorly-corrected 14mm f/2.8 for star shapes, even if the 14mm collects slightly more light per frame.

24mm — 84.1° horizontal: tighter compositions and conjunctions

At 24mm, the horizontal field of view narrows to 84.1°. This is still a wide-angle perspective, but it introduces a fundamentally different compositional approach to astrophotography. Instead of showing the whole arc of the Milky Way, you are now selecting a section of it — framing the galactic core with a specific foreground, or isolating a region of the sky with particular interest.

This tighter framing is advantageous in several situations. Planetary conjunctions — when two planets, a planet and the Moon, or a planet and a bright star appear close together — are often best photographed in the 24–35mm range, where the relevant celestial objects are large enough to be clearly visible while the surrounding sky still provides context. A 14mm shot of a Venus-Jupiter conjunction shows two points of light in a vast field of sky; a 24mm shot shows the same conjunction with more visual weight.

The 500-rule limit on full frame at 24mm is approximately 20.8 seconds. This is still adequate for single-frame Milky Way work at fast apertures, but it requires careful attention to aperture and ISO. On APS-C at 1.5×, the limit drops to about 13.9 seconds — at this point, f/1.4 becomes essentially mandatory if you want clean images at ISO 3200 rather than 6400 or higher.

The optical quality argument also favors 24mm. Coma and sagittal astigmatism are easier to control at this focal length, and premium lenses — the Sony FE 24mm f/1.4 GM is the benchmark — produce extremely clean star shapes across the full frame even wide open. The 24mm is also the preferred focal length for astrophotographers who shoot with a tracker and want to include detailed foregrounds in their compositions, because the tighter coverage allows more deliberate framing of both sky and ground elements.

Aperture: the most critical variable

In daytime photography, aperture affects depth of field and diffraction, and it is one of several levers you can adjust freely. In fixed-tripod astrophotography, aperture is the single most important specification on the lens, because it directly determines how much light you collect before the 500 rule requires you to close the shutter.

The relationship is straightforward: aperture area scales as the square of the f-number ratio. An f/1.4 lens has an aperture area four times larger than an f/2.8 lens at the same focal length. Four times more light means you can use either one-quarter the ISO (two stops less noise) or one-quarter the exposure time (which may allow shooting situations where trailing would otherwise be unacceptable). In practical terms, the difference between f/1.4 and f/2.8 is often the difference between a clean ISO 3200 image and a noisy ISO 12800 image with the same exposure length. That difference in image quality is immediately visible and significant.

This is why lens specifications matter so much in astrophotography compared to other genres. An f/1.4 aperture is not a minor convenience — it is a two-stop technical advantage that directly affects whether the final image is publishable or compromised by noise. The price premium for f/1.4 over f/2.8 optics is real and sometimes substantial, but it is also one of the most impactful investments a night sky photographer can make.

The corollary is that the fastest lens only helps if it controls aberrations at wide-open apertures. A theoretically f/1.4 lens that produces severe coma at f/1.4 and requires stopping down to f/2.8 for acceptable star shapes is, in practical terms, an f/2.8 lens — you have paid for maximum aperture you cannot use. Always verify coma performance at wide apertures before purchasing a lens specifically for astrophotography.

Sensor size and noise

Full-frame sensors have a significant advantage for astrophotography over smaller formats, and the reason is physical rather than just pixel-count related. When two sensors of different sizes have the same megapixel count, the full-frame sensor has larger individual photosites — the light-collecting wells for each pixel. Larger photosites accumulate more photons before the signal is swamped by electronic noise, which means they produce cleaner results at high ISO settings.

In astrophotography, where ISO 3200 is a conservative starting point and ISO 6400 or 12800 is common, this advantage is not marginal. A full-frame sensor at ISO 6400 will typically outperform an APS-C sensor at the same ISO by a clearly visible margin in shadow noise and fine detail in dimly lit star fields. The Milky Way's faint outer arms and dust lanes — the structures that give great Milky Way images their depth — are precisely the features that disappear first into noise on smaller sensors at high ISO.

For APS-C shooters, the implications for focal length choice are twofold. First, the crop factor (1.5× for Nikon/Sony, 1.6× for Canon) means you need a shorter focal length to match the field of view of a full-frame lens — a 10mm on APS-C gives roughly the same coverage as 15mm on full frame. Second, the shorter 500-rule limits on APS-C (because the effective focal length is longer) push even harder toward fast apertures. If you are shooting Milky Way on APS-C, prioritizing an f/1.4 or f/1.8 lens is more urgent than it is on full frame.

Coma and sagittal astigmatism

Two optical aberrations matter most for astrophotography, and both are worst in the corners of fast ultra-wide lenses.

Coma causes off-axis point sources to smear radially outward from the center of the frame, producing a comet-like or flame shape. In astrophotography, a coma-affected star in the corner of the frame is immediately obvious: instead of a tight point, it spreads into a small blob with a tail pointing away from the image center. The severity of coma depends on the lens design and the aperture — most lenses show less coma stopped down by one or two stops, though this also reduces the already limited light-gathering advantage of the fast aperture.

Sagittal astigmatism causes stars to render as short lines or crosses rather than points, oriented tangentially around the image center. It is less visually obvious than coma but contributes to the general impression that corners look "smeared" or "busy" rather than containing clean star fields. Like coma, it is worst wide open and at the edges of the frame.

The practical implication is that not all fast ultra-wide lenses are equally useful for astrophotography. A lens that appears excellent in daytime resolution charts — which typically evaluate center sharpness or even-illumination — may still show severe coma at f/2.8 that makes it unsuitable for star fields. Specialized reviews that test coma performance at wide apertures are more informative than general lens reviews for this application. Sites and videos that specifically photograph star fields with the lens wide open provide the most directly relevant data.

Star trackers: when the rules change

A star tracker is a motorized mount that rotates the camera opposite to the Earth's rotation, keeping stars stationary on the sensor regardless of exposure length. With a tracker, the 500-rule constraint effectively disappears for the sky — you can expose for 60, 120, or 300 seconds without star trailing, collecting vastly more light and enabling much lower ISO settings or much greater tonal depth.

Trackers change the focal length calculus in important ways. Longer focal lengths become more viable — 35mm, 50mm, or even longer lenses can be used for tracked wide-field shots that would be impossible from a fixed tripod. The exposure flexibility also reduces the aperture premium: if you can expose for 3 minutes instead of 25 seconds, an f/2.8 lens collects the same total light as an f/1.4 lens in about 45 seconds. The frantic push toward the fastest possible aperture relaxes considerably.

There is one important limitation: at very wide focal lengths on a tracker, field rotation becomes a concern. A tracker rotates around the celestial pole, which means stars at the edges of a very wide frame trace slight arcs rather than holding perfectly still, particularly away from the meridian or at high declinations. For 14mm fields, field rotation over long exposures can introduce smearing at the frame edges even with tracking. At 20–24mm, the effect is less pronounced over typical exposure lengths. Narrower fields — 35mm and longer — are largely unaffected by field rotation in single frames of moderate duration.

For photographers investing in a tracker, the 20–24mm range often becomes the preferred wide-field focal length: wide enough for expansive Milky Way compositions, narrow enough that field rotation is manageable, and optically well-corrected at the moderate apertures that become sufficient once tracking is available.

Practical recommendations

  • Shooting the full Milky Way arc on a fixed tripod, full frame: 14mm f/2.8 or faster. Accept some corner coma or budget for a premium option. The Samyang/Rokinon 14mm f/2.8 is the most accessible entry point.
  • Best single lens for Milky Way work, full frame: 20mm f/1.4. The Sigma 20mm f/1.4 DG DN Art is the reference choice. The extra stop of aperture over f/2.8 makes a measurable difference in image quality.
  • APS-C on a tight budget: Prioritize aperture over focal length. An f/1.4 lens at 16mm on APS-C is more useful than an f/2.8 at 10mm for most night sky work.
  • Planetary conjunctions or selective Milky Way framing: 24mm f/1.4. Tighter framing, excellent optical quality available, and corners remain manageable wide open on premium lenses.
  • Using a tracker: Aperture becomes less critical. Optical quality at moderate apertures (f/2–f/2.8) and focal lengths from 20–35mm become the priority.
