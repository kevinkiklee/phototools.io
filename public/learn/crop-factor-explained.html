Crop Factor Explained

What sensor size actually does to your field of view — and what it leaves completely alone.

Crop factor is one of those concepts that photographers talk about constantly but rarely explain with precision. The number itself is simple: it is the ratio of a full-frame sensor's diagonal (43.27mm) to the diagonal of the sensor in question. A sensor with a diagonal of 28.85mm has a crop factor of 43.27 ÷ 28.85 = 1.5×. That is the entire formula.

The name "crop factor" is a reasonable description of what happens optically. A lens projects a circular image onto the camera — the image circle. A full-frame sensor captures more of that circle. A smaller sensor captures only the central portion, which looks like you cropped into the full-frame image. A 1.5× crop factor means the sensor captures an area 1.5 times narrower than a full-frame sensor would.

Sensor sizes and their crop factors

These are the six sensor formats you will encounter in interchangeable-lens cameras, from largest to smallest:

APS-C (Nikon/Sony)23.5×15.6mm28.2mm1.5×

Micro Four Thirds17.3×13mm21.6mm2.0×
1" Sensor13.2×8.8mm15.9mm2.7×

Medium format has a crop factor below 1.0 because its sensor is larger than full-frame, capturing a wider slice of the image circle. Everything else on the list captures less than a full-frame sensor would.

How crop factor changes field of view

A lens's focal length is a fixed optical property — it does not change when you put the lens on a different camera body. A 50mm lens is always a 50mm lens. What changes is how much of the scene that lens captures when it projects onto a smaller sensor.

To find the full-frame equivalent field of view, multiply the actual focal length by the crop factor. A 50mm lens on a 1.5× APS-C body delivers the same angle of view as a 75mm lens on full frame. The scene appears narrower, objects appear larger in the frame — but the lens itself has not changed.

This matters when comparing cameras across systems. If you shoot a 35mm f/1.8 on a Sony APS-C body, you are getting the field of view of a 52mm lens — practically a normal lens, not the wide-angle the 35mm designation might suggest. The same 35mm on a Micro Four Thirds camera gives you a 70mm equivalent — solidly in portrait territory.

What crop factor does not change

Three things remain identical regardless of sensor size, and confusing them with crop factor is the source of most bad advice you will read online.

The actual focal length. A 50mm lens on an APS-C camera is still a 50mm lens. Its optical formula, its minimum focus distance, its flange-to-film distance — none of that changes. Only the field of view rendered by that sensor changes.

Aperture and light-gathering ability. f/2.8 admits the same amount of light per unit area onto the sensor regardless of sensor size. A smaller sensor at f/2.8 simply collects light over a smaller physical area, which is why total light gathered is less — but the f-number is not wrong or misleading. Exposure is determined by f-stop and shutter speed, not sensor size. A photo taken at 1/500s, f/4, ISO 400 will have the same exposure on a phone sensor as on medium format.

The optics of the lens itself. Distortion characteristics, chromatic aberration, bokeh rendering — these come from the lens design, not the sensor. A 50mm f/1.8 will render out-of-focus backgrounds the same way on any body it is mounted on. The aperture blades are the same physical size, the glass elements are the same.

Crop factor and depth of field — a more careful look

Here is where precision matters. Crop factor does not directly change depth of field at a given aperture, subject distance, and focal length. If you photograph the same subject at the same distance with the same 50mm f/2 lens, the optical depth of field is identical — regardless of whether the sensor is full frame or APS-C. What differs is the framing: the APS-C image shows less of the scene, as if you cropped in.

The confusion arises because photographers rarely hold distance constant. To get the same framing on an APS-C body as on full frame, you either step back or use a shorter focal length. Step back, and depth of field increases (distance is the dominant DoF variable). Use a shorter focal length, and depth of field also increases. So in practice, when you make images that are compositionally equivalent across sensor formats, the smaller sensor tends to produce deeper depth of field. But that is a consequence of framing decisions, not a direct optical effect of the sensor itself.

The shorthand "APS-C gives shallower or deeper depth of field" is imprecise. What is precise: to achieve the same framing and depth of field on APS-C as on full frame, you need a lens with a shorter focal length and a wider aperture. To match 50mm f/1.8 on full frame with a 1.5× APS-C body, you would need approximately a 33mm f/1.2 — a significantly harder lens to make and more expensive to buy.

Practical takeaways

  • Crop factor is a description of sensor coverage, not a physical modification to your lens.
  • Multiply your focal length by the crop factor to find the full-frame equivalent angle of view.
  • Smaller sensors favor telephoto reach — the crop factor amplifies effective reach at no additional cost.
  • Full-frame and medium format favor wide-angle photography and shallow depth of field work, where the larger sensor captures a wider field without requiring ultra-short focal lengths.
  • When a manufacturer specifies "35mm equivalent" in a camera spec sheet, they have already done the multiplication. The actual focal length of the lens is a different, shorter number.

The next time you pick up a new camera body, the first question worth asking is: what is the crop factor, and what does that do to the lenses I already own? A 24mm wide-angle becomes a 36mm standard lens on APS-C — still useful, but serving a different role. A 300mm telephoto becomes a 450mm reach — suddenly quite long. Knowing the crop factor tells you immediately what your glass will do on that body.
