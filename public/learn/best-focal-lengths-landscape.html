Best Focal Lengths for Landscape Photography

From ultra-wide drama to telephoto compression — how to choose the right focal length for the scene in front of you.

Landscape photography rewards deliberate lens choice more than almost any other genre. Unlike street or event work, where the moment determines your focal length, landscapes are largely static — you have time to consider what you want the image to say and choose your tool accordingly. The difference between a 14mm shot of a mountain range and a 50mm shot of the same mountain range is not just a matter of cropping; the two images will look entirely different in terms of spatial relationships, foreground prominence, and the sense of scale they convey.

This guide covers the focal lengths most useful for landscape work on a full-frame sensor (36×24mm). APS-C equivalents are included for each range. All horizontal field-of-view figures use the standard rectilinear projection formula.

Quick reference: focal lengths for landscape work

Focal Length Horizontal FOV (FF) APS-C Equivalent Best For Filter Consideration

24mm 84° ~16mm All-purpose landscape standard, travel, filter-friendly Works with standard 77mm circular filters
35mm 63° ~23mm Forests, waterfalls, intimate scenes with defined subject Works with standard 77mm circular filters
50–85mm 47°–29° ~33–56mm Mountain ridgelines, desert dunes, layer stacking Standard circular filters; slim ring recommended to avoid vignetting

14–20mm ultra-wide: 114°–95° horizontal

Ultra-wide lenses are the first tool most landscape photographers reach for, and for good reason — they are extremely capable in the right circumstances. At 14mm, your horizontal field of view is 114°, which is wider than comfortable human peripheral vision. The frame encompasses an enormous slice of the environment, and objects close to the lens appear disproportionately large relative to objects farther away.

That foreground-to-background scale is the defining characteristic and primary appeal of ultra-wide landscape photography. A rock formation two feet from your lens will loom large in the frame while a mountain range miles behind it still fits comfortably. This dramatic compression of scale — where near things are huge and far things are still visible — creates a sense of depth and immersion that is essentially impossible to replicate with longer focal lengths in post-processing.

The applications are extensive: mountain panoramas where you want the sweep of an alpine scene from foreground boulders to distant peaks; canyon overlooks where the sheer scale of the geology demands maximum coverage; Northern Lights photography, where you want to show both the aurora filling the sky and the landscape beneath it; and coastal scenes where breaking waves close to the lens anchor a wide horizon behind them.

The significant risk with ultra-wide lenses is the "empty center" problem. When there is no compelling foreground element close to the lens, the dramatic perspective of a 14–20mm frame works against you. The subject shrinks in the distance, the sky and empty ground dominate, and the image lacks a visual anchor. Before shooting ultra-wide, ask whether there is strong foreground interest within 6–10 feet of the lens. Tide pools, wildflowers, cracked earth, leading lines of rock — anything that fills the lower third with deliberate detail will make the image work. Without it, a 24mm or 35mm will likely produce a stronger result.

APS-C equivalents: On a 1.5× crop body (Nikon/Sony APS-C), a 10mm lens gives the same field of view as a 15mm on full frame. The Canon APS-C 1.6× crop makes this approximately 9mm for a 14mm equivalent. Dedicated APS-C ultra-wide lenses — such as Nikon's 10–20mm and Sony's 10–18mm — are purpose-built for this range and generally outperform full-frame ultra-wides on crop sensors at the edges.

24mm: the versatile landscape standard

If you own one wide-angle lens and shoot landscapes, it should probably be a 24mm. The 84° horizontal field of view is wide enough to show sweeping scenes and give strong foreground context, but the perspective rendering is far more controlled than 14–20mm. Edges stay honest, straight lines do not visibly bow, and subjects at normal distances appear natural rather than distorted.

The practical advantages of 24mm for landscape work extend beyond optics. It is the longest focal length that still delivers the broad, immersive coverage that distinguishes landscape photography from general photography — and it is the shortest focal length that works cleanly with standard 77mm or 82mm circular filters. ND filters, graduated NDs, and polarizers are essential tools for serious landscape work. At 14–20mm, you need large and expensive 150mm rectangular filter systems; at 24mm, inexpensive threaded circular filters mount directly to the lens without vignetting.

Hyperfocal distance is also favorable at 24mm. At f/11, everything from approximately 1 meter to infinity falls within acceptable depth of field using the standard circle-of-confusion criterion for a full-frame sensor. In practice, this means you can focus roughly 1.5–2 meters from the lens, stop down to f/8–f/11, and have confident front-to-back sharpness without precise focus calculations. Ultra-wide lenses make hyperfocal shooting even easier, but 24mm hits the sweet spot: hyperfocal distances are short enough to be practical, and diffraction from extreme stopping-down is not yet a significant concern.

Recommended starting point for any landscape photographer. If you own a kit zoom, the 24mm end is worth treating as a prime and learning deliberately.

APS-C equivalent: 16mm on a 1.5× body; 15mm on Canon 1.6×. Sony's 16mm f/2.8 and Sigma's 16mm f/1.4 are popular options. The Fujifilm XF 16mm f/1.4 is the reference ultra-wide prime for Fuji APS-C shooters.

35mm: tight compositions and intimate landscapes

At 63° horizontal, 35mm occupies a different creative space from 24mm. It is no longer sweeping — you cannot show everything. Instead, it forces compositional decisions. You must identify the specific feature of a landscape that merits attention and frame around it deliberately.

This is where 35mm excels: forest scenes, where you want to show a stand of trees without the lens distortion that makes trunks in ultra-wide shots appear to lean away from the center; waterfall compositions, where the fall itself is the subject and context is secondary; intimate landscapes where the interest is a particular rock, a stream bend, or a section of dunes rather than the panoramic whole. The 35mm perspective also handles fog and low-visibility conditions better than wider lenses — when the background disappears into mist, a wider lens simply shows more mist, while 35mm can frame a foreground subject that stands apart from the ethereal background.

The tighter coverage of 35mm also reduces the sky-to-ground ratio compared to ultra-wide shots, which can be either an advantage (fewer clouds needed to fill the frame) or a disadvantage (epic cloudscapes are harder to capture) depending on conditions.

APS-C equivalent: 23–24mm on a 1.5× body. Fujifilm's XF 23mm f/1.4 and the XF 23mm f/2 WR are designed around this equivalence on the Fuji system. On Sony APS-C, the 24mm f/1.8 G delivers essentially the same angle of view.

50–85mm telephoto landscapes: the overlooked range

Telephoto lenses are almost never mentioned in beginner landscape photography guides, yet many of the most compelling landscape images in editorial and fine-art contexts were made with lenses in the 50–85mm range. The reason is compression.

Telephoto lenses do not create optical compression — they are simply being used from farther away, and the greater subject distance changes how near and far objects appear sized relative to each other. The result is that distant features appear larger relative to the foreground than they do in an ultra-wide shot of the same scene. A mountain range photographed at 85mm from a ridge looks more imposing and more densely stacked than the same range at 24mm. Desert dunes shot at 70mm show the sinuous layering of ridgelines in a way that is impossible at 24mm without walking several miles closer.

The technique that telephoto compression enables in landscapes is layer stacking: using the apparent size increase of distant elements to create visual depth through overlapping planes. Mountain ridgelines seen across a valley, where three or four successive ridges overlap and decrease in value toward the horizon, are one of the classic subjects for this approach. Desert dunes with their undulating patterns of light and shadow, coastal headlands viewed from a distance, and tree lines on rolling hills are all subjects that benefit from the compression and selective inclusion that 50–85mm provides.

Another advantage of this range for landscape work is its alignment with golden-hour and blue-hour shooting. At 85mm, you can isolate a setting sun partially obscured by distant mountains, or frame a rising moon against a ridge at dusk — compositions that require the apparent enlargement of distant features that only a longer focal length provides.

APS-C equivalents: A 35mm lens on a 1.5× crop body delivers 52mm equivalent; a 50mm delivers approximately 75mm equivalent. The standard 50mm f/1.8 on any APS-C system acts as a modest telephoto suitable for landscape compression work.

Filters: practical considerations by focal length

Landscape photography relies on filters in a way that most other genres do not. ND filters allow long exposures that smooth water and clouds in daylight. Graduated NDs balance bright sky against darker foreground. Polarizers deepen sky color, remove surface reflections from water, and reduce atmospheric haze.

The filter system you can use is directly constrained by your focal length. Ultra-wide lenses at 14–20mm generally require a 150mm rectangular filter system — a holder that mounts to the front of the lens and accepts large glass or resin panels. These systems cost $150–$400 for the holder alone, and quality 150mm filter panels add $100–$400 each. The investment is substantial.

At 24mm and longer, standard 77mm or 82mm threaded circular filters work without vignetting. A quality circular polarizer, a 3-stop ND, and a 6-stop ND in 77mm cost a fraction of the 150mm system equivalent and fit in a small pouch. This is one of the practical reasons that 24mm is the most recommended starting point for landscape filter work — the system cost is dramatically lower while still covering the most versatile focal length for the genre.

Hyperfocal distance: a quick guide

Getting front-to-back sharpness is a core technical requirement for landscape photography. Hyperfocal distance — the focus distance at which everything from half that distance to infinity is acceptably sharp — varies significantly with focal length.

At 24mm f/11, the hyperfocal distance on a full-frame sensor is approximately 1.8 meters. Focus at that distance and everything from 90cm to infinity is sharp. In practice, focusing on an object about 5–6 feet from the lens at f/11 delivers reliable front-to-back sharpness for most landscape compositions.

At 14mm f/11, the hyperfocal distance drops to about 0.6 meters — meaning almost everything in the frame is sharp regardless of where you focus, simply because the field of view is so wide. This is one of the reasons ultra-wide lenses are forgiving for beginners: the depth-of-field is enormous even at moderate apertures.

At 35mm f/11, the hyperfocal distance is around 3.7 meters. You need to be more deliberate about focus placement. At 50mm f/11, it rises to approximately 7.5 meters — at this point, foreground elements closer than about 4 meters will begin to show softness if you focus at infinity. Stopping down to f/16 helps at 50mm, though diffraction begins to soften fine detail above f/11–f/16 on full-frame sensors.

Choosing for the scene

No single focal length is correct for all landscape work. A few practical heuristics:

  • Strong foreground interest within 6 feet of the lens? Use 14–20mm to exploit the dramatic scale relationship between near and far.
  • Want broad coverage without extreme distortion? 24mm is the default. It handles filters, hyperfocal focusing, and perspective well in nearly every landscape situation.
  • Subject is a specific feature — a waterfall, a stand of trees, a rock formation? 35mm gives tighter, more deliberate framing without distortion.
  • Distant mountains, layered ridgelines, desert dunes? 50–85mm compresses the scene and makes distant features appear more imposing.
  • On an APS-C body? Your 16mm acts like 24mm, your 24mm acts like 36mm. Adjust choices accordingly — you may need a dedicated wide prime to access the ultra-wide range.

The most common mistake in landscape photography is assuming that wider is always better. Ultra-wide lenses are powerful but specific tools. A well-composed 35mm landscape with a clear subject and deliberate negative space will outperform an unfocused 14mm snapshot every time.
