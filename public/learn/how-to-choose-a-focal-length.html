How to Choose a Focal Length

A practical decision framework — from genre and sensor to primes, zooms, and budget.

Choosing a focal length is one of the most consequential decisions you make as a photographer, yet the advice you find online tends toward the vague: "it depends on what you shoot." This guide turns that non-answer into a structured framework. Work through the five steps below and you will arrive at a concrete starting point — not a theoretical ideal, but the lens most likely to serve you well given what you actually photograph and what you can reasonably spend.

All focal length ranges in this article refer to full-frame equivalent. If you shoot APS-C, Micro Four Thirds, or another format, the final step shows you how to translate those numbers to the actual focal lengths you need.

Step 1 — What do you shoot most?

Genre is the single most reliable predictor of which focal length range you need. Different subjects require different working distances, different perspective rendering, and different relationships between foreground and background. The table below maps the most common genres to recommended focal length ranges on full frame and their APS-C equivalents.

Genre Full Frame (FF) APS-C (1.5× crop) Why this range
Landscape 14–35mm 10–24mm Wide field of view captures sweeping scenes; foreground context tells a fuller story
Portrait 50–135mm 35–85mm Short telephoto renders faces with flattering perspective; distance keeps subjects comfortable
Street 28–50mm 18–35mm Near-natural perspective blends into scenes without drawing attention; easy to work quickly
Wildlife 200–600mm 135–400mm Animals rarely allow close approach; telephoto reach is non-negotiable
Astrophotography 14–24mm 10–16mm Wide coverage captures more sky; short focal length allows longer exposures without star trails
Events & weddings 24–70mm 16–50mm Zoom range covers both environmental context and tighter moments without a lens change
Sports & action 70–200mm+ 50–135mm+ Telephoto compresses the playing field and reaches athletes at a distance

If you shoot across multiple genres, look for overlap. A photographer who does both portraits and street work will find that a 35mm prime on full frame covers both adequately — slightly wide for portraits but perfectly fine for documentary street work. A landscape and astrophotography shooter is well served by a single 16–24mm prime.

Step 2 — What sensor do you have?

The focal length printed on a lens is a fixed optical property. What changes with sensor size is the field of view that lens delivers on your body. A 50mm lens on a Sony APS-C camera (1.5× crop factor) gives the same angle of view as a 75mm lens on full frame — that is, a short telephoto, not a normal lens.

To find the focal length you actually need on your body, divide the full-frame focal length by your crop factor:

  • APS-C (Nikon, Sony, Fuji) — 1.5× crop: divide by 1.5. Want 50mm FF equivalent? Buy a 35mm.
  • APS-C (Canon) — 1.6× crop: divide by 1.6. Want 85mm FF equivalent? Buy a 50mm.
  • Micro Four Thirds — 2.0× crop: divide by 2. Want 200mm FF equivalent? A 100mm delivers it.
  • Medium format — 0.79× crop: multiply by 0.79. A 35mm on MF is equivalent to a 28mm on FF.

The crop factor can work in your favor for wildlife and sports photographers: a 300mm lens on a 1.5× APS-C body delivers the field of view of a 450mm lens on full frame, at a fraction of the cost and weight of a true 450mm prime. Conversely, landscape and astro photographers on APS-C need to buy genuinely shorter focal lengths — a 10mm or 12mm ultra-wide — to achieve the sweeping coverage that 14–16mm delivers on full frame.

Step 3 — Prime or zoom?

Every focal length discussion eventually arrives at this question. The trade-off is real and the right answer depends on how you work, not on abstract specifications.

Primes

A prime lens has a fixed focal length. That constraint forces you to move your feet — you zoom with your legs rather than a ring — which many photographers find improves their compositional discipline. In exchange you get three practical benefits:

  • Wider maximum aperture. A 50mm f/1.8 prime admits roughly four times more light than a 24–70mm f/2.8 zoom at 50mm. That gap matters in low light and for shallow depth of field work.
  • Smaller physical size. A 35mm f/1.8 prime is pocketable. A 24–105mm zoom is not. For street work, travel, and any situation where camera presence matters, primes disappear.
  • Lower cost. A 50mm f/1.8 on virtually every major camera system costs between $100 and $250. The equivalent reach and aperture from a zoom would cost ten times more — if it were possible at all.

Zooms

A zoom lens covers a range of focal lengths in one barrel. The right zoom used well is not a compromise — it is the right tool for certain situations:

  • Unpredictable subjects. Wedding photographers, photojournalists, and event photographers often cannot reposition. A 24–70mm f/2.8 or 70–200mm f/2.8 lets them reframe without moving.
  • Travel minimalism. One 24–105mm lens covers more scenarios than three primes with fewer weight and bag-space penalties.
  • Learning before committing. A versatile kit zoom is an excellent way to discover which focal length you reach for most — and to confirm that before buying a prime.

The practical trade-off: most zoom lenses max out at f/2.8 on the fast end, and f/4–f/6.3 for consumer-grade options. If you shoot in dim environments or want pronounced background blur, primes will deliver what zooms cannot.

Step 4 — What is your budget?

Budget constrains options but rarely leaves you stuck. Every major camera system — Canon RF, Nikon Z, Sony E, Fuji X, Micro Four Thirds — offers a fast 50mm equivalent prime for under $250. This lens category is the correct starting point for most photographers adding their first prime.

Here is a rough tier structure for how lens investment scales with quality:

  • Under $300 — entry prime: 50mm f/1.8 on your system. Optically excellent, physically small, immediately useful. Buy this first.
  • $300–800 — mid-range prime or kit zoom upgrade: 35mm f/1.8, 85mm f/1.8, or a quality 24–70mm f/4. These are refined tools with better build quality and superior edge performance.
  • $800–2,000 — professional primes: 24mm f/1.4, 85mm f/1.4, 135mm f/1.8. Meaningful optical improvements at wide apertures. Justified when your work demands the performance.
  • $2,000+ — fast zooms and super-telephotos: 70–200mm f/2.8, 300mm f/2.8, 500mm f/4. These are specialist tools. Rent before buying to confirm you need the capability.

Renting is significantly underused by photographers building their kit. A $2,000 lens can typically be rented for $80–150 per day. Three rental sessions — shooting your actual subjects in your actual conditions — will tell you more about whether you need that lens than any review article.

Step 5 — Start with one, then specialize

The most common mistake in lens buying is acquiring too many lenses too quickly, before you understand what each one does for your specific subjects. The result is a bag full of glass and uncertainty about which lens to reach for.

A better approach: start with the single lens that best covers your primary genre, and commit to shooting with only that lens for at least three months. Constraints breed clarity. After ninety days you will know exactly what that lens does well, where it falls short, and what focal length you consistently wish you had. That knowledge is worth more than any buyer's guide.

A practical starting kit that covers most photographers:

  1. First lens: 50mm f/1.8 (or equivalent for your sensor). Versatile, affordable, optically capable.
  2. Second lens: determined by what the 50mm cannot do for your subjects. More reach? An 85mm or 135mm. More width? A 24mm or 35mm.
  3. Third lens: a specialist — ultra-wide for astro/landscape, a fast telephoto for wildlife/sports, a macro if you shoot close-up work.
